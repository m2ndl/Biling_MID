1. Key Concepts Covered

  • Second Language Acquisition (SLA):
    • Core Definition: The systematic process of learning languages beyond the mother tongue (L1).
    • Distinctions (from Gass et al., 2013, p. 4-5):
      • Second Language (L2): Learning a nonnative language in the environment where it is spoken (e.g., learning English in the US).
      • Foreign Language (FL): Learning a nonnative language in the environment of one's native language (e.g., learning English in France).
    • Acquisition vs. Learning: 'Acquisition' (subconscious "picking up") vs. 'Learning' (conscious, "knowing about" language).
  • Bilingualism: The use of two or more languages in daily life. Viewed as a continuum (Gass et al., 2013, p. 479).
  • Multilingualism: The global norm of speaking more than two languages.
  • Types of Bilingualism:
    • Simultaneous vs. Sequential (or Successive): Learning two languages from birth vs. learning an L2 after the L1 is established.
    • Compound vs. Coordinate: An older distinction. Current research (e.g., Cook's "multicompetence") suggests L1 and L2 systems are interconnected in a single mind.
    • Additive vs. Subtractive: Learning an L2 while maintaining and valuing the L1 (Additive) vs. learning an L2 that replaces or weakens the L1 (Subtractive).

2. Historical Development & Key Theories

  • Behaviorist Theory (1950s-1960s):
    • Core Idea: Language learning is habit formation through imitation, practice, and reinforcement (Gass et al., 2013, p. 80-83).
    • Role of L1: L1 habits are transferred. Negative transfer (interference) was seen as the major source of errors.
    • Associated Practice: The Contrastive Analysis Hypothesis (CAH).
  • Mentalist Theory (1960s-1970s):
    • Core Idea: A rejection of Behaviorism. Learning is a creative, cognitive process driven by an innate Language Acquisition Device (LAD) and Universal Grammar (UG).
    • Evidence: Children's creative errors (e.g., *comed*, *go-ed*) prove they are creating rules, not just imitating.
  • Interactionist Theory (1980s-present):
    • Core Idea: A middle-ground approach. Internal mental processes are activated by social interaction. Language is acquired through communicating.
    • Key Concept: Negotiation of meaning during communication breakdowns is crucial.
  • Usage-based Theory (1990s-present):
    • Core Idea: An emergentist approach (Gass et al., 2013, p. 272-273). Rejects innate UG.
    • Mechanism: Learners learn language by "extracting regularities from the input." Grammar is "emergent" and based on frequency and exemplars.

3. Key Statistics

  • Monolingualism is the global minority condition.
  • Multilingualism provides economic advantages in globalized markets.

1. Key Concepts Covered

  • Cognitive Architecture: How are L1 and L2 represented in the mind? Cook's (2003) concept of multicompetence suggests an interconnected system.
  • Executive Control: Higher-level cognitive processes (attention, inhibition, task switching). Bilinguals often show advantages ('bilingual advantage').
  • Working Memory (WM): System for temporarily holding and manipulating information (Gass et al., 2013, p. 267).
    • Baddeley's Model: Includes the phonological loop (verbal), visuospatial sketchpad (visual), and central executive (attentional supervisor).
    • Phonological Short-Term Memory (PSTM): Component linked to successful L2 vocabulary and grammar learning.
  • Metalinguistic Awareness: The ability to consciously reflect on and manipulate language. Bilingual children often show "superior abilities in judging grammatical accuracy" (Bialystok, cited in Gass et al., 2013, p. 482).
  • Critical Period Hypothesis (CPH): The theory that age affects language learning (Gass et al., 2013, p. 434). A "limited developmental period" often linked to puberty. A Sensitive Period is a more gradual alternative.

2. Cognitive Benefits & Brain Organization

  • Cognitive Benefits: Increased creative and divergent thinking; enhanced executive function; superior metalinguistic knowledge.
  • Neural Plasticity & Brain Organization: The brain's ability to change in response to L2 learning.
    • L1-L2 overlap: L1 and L2 use shared and distinct neural regions.
    • Proficiency effects: A learner's competence level can impact brain structure.
  • Cognitive Reserve: The idea that bilingualism helps build the brain's resilience against age-related cognitive decline.

3. Individual Cognitive Differences

  • Language Aptitude: A specific talent for language learning. Measured by the MLAT. (See Carroll's Model).
  • Working memory capacity effects (from PDF summary)
  • Learning Strategies: Conscious actions learners take (Gass et al., 2013, p. 468).
    • Metacognitive Strategies: (e.g., planning, monitoring one's learning).
    • Cognitive Strategies: (e.g., repetition, note-taking).
    • Social/Affective Strategies: (e.g., asking for clarification, managing anxiety).
  • Intelligence: General cognitive ability.

1. Key Concepts Covered

  • Sociocultural Theory (SCT) Concepts:
    • Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD): (Vygotsky) The distance between independent problem solving and potential development with guidance (Gass et al., 2013, p. 295).
    • Scaffolding: Support provided by a More Knowledgeable Other (MKO) within the ZPD.
    • Mediation: The core Vygotskyan concept that we do not interact with the world directly; our thought and action are "mediated" (shaped and organized) by cultural tools.
      • Tools: Can be physical (e.g., a pencil, a computer) or symbolic.
      • Language as the Key Tool: Language is the most powerful symbolic tool used to organize thought, plan actions, and share culture.
      • Example: A teacher explaining a grammar rule is using language to *mediate* a student's understanding of that abstract concept.
    • Regulation: Moves from Object-regulation -> Other-regulation (scaffolding) -> Self-regulation (via Private Speech).
  • Communities of Practice (CoP): (Lave & Wenger) Groups defined by shared practices. Learning involves moving from Legitimate Peripheral Participation (LPP) to full participation.
  • Investment vs. Motivation: (Bonny Norton) Argues learners 'invest' in an L2 expecting a 'return,' influenced by Identity and Power Relations.
  • Power Relations: Social hierarchies affect learners' access to interactions and opportunities to speak.

1. Key Concepts: Ideologies & LPP

  • Language Ideologies: Beliefs about languages linked to power (Baker & Wright, 2017, p. 102).
    • Standard Language Ideology: Belief that one "standard" variety is superior, devaluing others.
    • Monolingual Ideology: Belief that monolingualism is the norm or ideal.
  • Language Policy (and Planning) (LPP): Explicit decisions about language use.
    • Status Planning: Changing the function of a language (e.g., official language).
    • Corpus Planning: Changing the structure of a language (e.g., standardizing spelling).

2. Educational Models

  • Transitional (TBE):
    • Goal: Assimilation into L2-only classrooms; *not* bilingualism.
    • Method: "Weak," subtractive model. L1 is used as a temporary bridge to learn the L2 (Baker & Wright, 2017).
    • Outcome: L1 support is removed once L2 proficiency is deemed sufficient. Often leads to L1 loss.
  • Maintenance (or Developmental):
    • Goal: Full bilingualism and biliteracy for language minority students.
    • Method: "Strong," additive model. Students receive instruction in *both* L1 and L2 for many years.
    • Outcome: Values and develops the L1 alongside the L2.
  • Two-Way Immersion (TWI) (or Dual Language):
    • Goal: Full bilingualism and biliteracy for *all* students.
    • Method: "Strong," additive model. Integrates language minority students (e.g., Spanish speakers) and language majority students (e.g., English speakers) in the same classroom.
    • Outcome: Both groups learn from each other, with all students becoming bilingual.
  • CLIL (Content and Language Integrated Learning):
    • Goal: To learn a subject *and* a language simultaneously.
    • Method: A form of immersion where subjects (e.g., biology, history) are taught *through* a non-native language.
    • Outcome: Content knowledge is gained while using the L2 in a meaningful, academic context.

Krashen's Monitor Model (The 5 Hypotheses)

This model (1980s) was highly influential, though not all parts are supported by modern research. (Gass et al., 2013, pp. 129-134; Ellis, 2015, p. 15, 26).

1. The Acquisition-Learning Hypothesis

This is the central hypothesis. It claims there are two independent systems:

  • Acquisition: The subconscious, "natural" process of "picking up" a language, just as children do with their L1. It's used for fluent communication.
  • Learning: The conscious, intentional process of "knowing about" a language (e.g., memorizing grammar rules).

2. The Monitor Hypothesis

This explains the relationship between the two systems. The "acquired" system is the source of all fluent output. The "learned" system acts only as an "inspector" or "Monitor" that checks and edits this output.

Three Conditions for Use: The Monitor can only be used when the learner:

  • Has sufficient Time.
  • Is Focused on Form (i.e., thinking about correctness).
  • Knows the Rule consciously.

3. The Natural Order Hypothesis

This claims that grammatical morphemes (e.g., plural *-s*, past tense *-ed*) are acquired in a predictable order, regardless of the learner's L1, age, or instruction.

4. The Input Hypothesis (Comprehensible Input)

This explains how acquisition happens. Acquisition occurs *only* when the learner receives comprehensible input (CI) that is slightly beyond their current level of competence (designated as $i+1$).

5. The Affective Filter Hypothesis

This explains why learners, even when given perfect "$i+1$" input, might not acquire it. Affective (emotional) factors like motivation, anxiety, and self-confidence create a "filter."

  • Low Filter: High motivation, low anxiety. Input passes through and becomes acquisition.
  • High Filter: Low motivation, high anxiety. Input is blocked, and acquisition does not occur.

Carroll's Model of Language Aptitude

This model defines L2 aptitude (natural talent) as a stable, cognitive trait composed of four specific abilities, measured by the Modern Language Aptitude Test (MLAT). (Gass et al., 2013, p. 445; Ellis, 2015, p. 53).

1. Phonemic Coding Ability

The ability to identify distinct sounds, form associations between sounds and written symbols, and retain these associations in memory.

2. Grammatical Sensitivity

The ability to recognize the grammatical function of words in a sentence (e.g., identifying the subject, verb, or object) without necessarily knowing the explicit rule.

3. Inductive Language-Learning Ability

The ability to identify patterns and infer rules from a given language sample, and to form new utterances based on those rules.

4. Rote-Learning Ability (Associative Memory)

The ability to form and remember associations between new words (or sounds) and their meanings, particularly in forming L1-L2 vocabulary links.

Lave & Wenger's Communities of Practice (CoP)

(Sourced from Ellis, 2015, p. 232)

Core Idea

Learning is not just a cognitive process but a social one of "situated learning." It is the process of becoming a member of a community that shares common practices.

Key Concept: Legitimate Peripheral Participation (LPP)

Newcomers ("novices") learn by first observing and performing simple, low-risk tasks on the "periphery" of the community. As they gain expertise and become more integrated, they move toward "full participation" in the community's practices.

Norton's Investment Theory

(Sourced from Ellis, 2015, p. 230)

Core Idea

Replaces the static concept of "motivation" with the more dynamic, socially-aware concept of "investment." Learners "invest" in an L2 with the expectation of a "return."

Key Components

  • Identity: A learner's investment is tied to their multiple, complex, and often contradictory social identities (e.g., mother, worker, immigrant, student).
  • Power: The learner's "right to speak" is negotiated in interactions. Power relations (e.g., between an immigrant and an employer) can determine whether a learner is given opportunities to speak or is silenced, thus affecting their learning.
  • Agency: Learners are not just passive recipients; they are agents who can accept, resist, or reshape their social roles and identities in the process of learning.

Cummins' BICS/CALP & CUP Hypotheses

(Sourced from Baker & Wright, 2017, pp. 154-156)

BICS (Basic Interpersonal Communicative Skills)

  • The "surface" language of social conversation (the "tip of the iceberg").
  • It is context-embedded (supported by gestures, facial expressions).
  • Not cognitively demanding.
  • Acquired relatively quickly (approx. 2 years).

CALP (Cognitive Academic Language Proficiency)

  • The abstract, "school" language needed for academic success (the "submerged" part).
  • It is context-reduced (e.g., reading a textbook, writing an essay).
  • Cognitively demanding.
  • Takes much longer to acquire (5-7+ years).

CUP (Common Underlying Proficiency) / The Interdependence Hypothesis

This is the "Dual Iceberg Model." It argues that while the BICS (surface fluency) of L1 and L2 are separate, the CALP (academic skills, literacy, abstract concepts) is shared in one common "engine."

Key Implication: Strengthening the CALP in the L1 (e.g., teaching a child to read in Spanish) will directly transfer to and strengthen the CALP in the L2 (e.g., English). This is the central argument for maintenance bilingual education.

Bourdieu's Sociological Concepts

(Sourced from Baker & Wright, 2017, pp. 103, 307)

Cultural Capital

Non-financial assets (e.g., education, ways of speaking, style of dress, cultural knowledge) that are valued by a society's dominant institutions, especially schools.

Habitus

The set of deep-seated, socially learned dispositions, habits, and attitudes that shape how an individual perceives and acts in the world.

Legitimate Language / Symbolic Power

Dominant groups impose their language variety (e.g., "standard" English) as the "legitimate" or "correct" language. This grants it "symbolic power," making other varieties (e.g., dialects, heritage languages) seem "illegitimate" and thus turning linguistic differences into social inequality.

Gardner's Socio-Educational Model

(Sourced from Gass et al., 2013, pp. 453-454; Ellis, 2015, pp. 59-60)

Core Idea

A foundational social-psychological model explaining L2 learning success.

Key Components

  • Integrativeness: The core concept. A positive disposition toward the L2 group and a genuine desire to interact with and even identify with them.
  • Attitudes toward the Learning Situation: The learner's feelings about the teacher, the course, and the learning environment.
  • Motivation (proper): The effort and desire to learn, combined with favorable attitudes.
  • Integrative Motivation: The overall construct combining Integrativeness, Attitudes, and Motivation/Effort.
  • Instrumental Orientation: A practical, utilitarian reason for learning (e.g., to get a job, pass a test, fulfill a requirement).

D√∂rnyei's L2 Motivational Self System

(Sourced from Ellis, 2015, pp. 53-54)

Core Idea

A modern cognitive model of motivation based on future self-guides. Motivation is generated by the discrepancy between a learner's "actual self" and their "future selves."

Key Components

  • 1. Ideal L2 Self: The person the learner wants to become (e.g., a fluent, successful L2 speaker). This is a powerful "promotional" motive.
  • 2. Ought-to L2 Self: The attributes the learner believes they should possess, based on external expectations (e.g., from parents, teachers) to avoid negative outcomes. This is a "preventative" motive.
  • 3. L2 Learning Experience: The immediate, situational motivation related to the learning environment itself (the teacher, the materials, feelings of success).
