1. Key Concepts Covered

  • Second Language Acquisition (SLA):
    • Core Definition: The systematic process of learning languages beyond the mother tongue (L1).
    • Distinctions (from Gass et al., 2013, p. 4-5):
      • Second Language (L2): Learning a nonnative language in the environment where it is spoken (e.g., learning English in the US).
      • Foreign Language (FL): Learning a nonnative language in the environment of one's native language (e.g., learning English in France).
    • Acquisition vs. Learning: 'Acquisition' (subconscious "picking up") vs. 'Learning' (conscious, "knowing about" language).
  • Bilingualism: The use of two or more languages in daily life. Viewed as a continuum (Gass et al., 2013, p. 479).
  • Multilingualism: The global norm of speaking more than two languages.
  • Types of Bilingualism:
    • Simultaneous vs. Sequential (or Successive): Learning two languages from birth vs. learning an L2 after the L1 is established.
    • Compound vs. Coordinate: An older distinction. Current research (e.g., Cook's "multicompetence") suggests L1 and L2 systems are interconnected in a single mind.
    • Additive vs. Subtractive: Learning an L2 while maintaining and valuing the L1 (Additive) vs. learning an L2 that replaces or weakens the L1 (Subtractive).

2. Historical Development & Key Theories

  • Behaviorist Theory (1950s-1960s):
    • Core Idea: Language learning is habit formation through imitation, practice, and reinforcement (Gass et al., 2013, p. 80-83).
    • Role of L1: L1 habits are transferred. Negative transfer (interference) was seen as the major source of errors.
    • Associated Practice: The Contrastive Analysis Hypothesis (CAH).
  • Mentalist Theory (1960s-1970s):
    • Core Idea: A rejection of Behaviorism. Learning is a creative, cognitive process driven by an innate Language Acquisition Device (LAD) and Universal Grammar (UG).
    • Evidence: Children's creative errors (e.g., *comed*, *go-ed*) prove they are creating rules, not just imitating.
  • Interactionist Theory (1980s-present):
    • Core Idea: A middle-ground approach. Internal mental processes are activated by social interaction. Language is acquired through communicating.
    • Key Concept: Negotiation of meaning during communication breakdowns is crucial.
  • Usage-based Theory (1990s-present):
    • Core Idea: An emergentist approach (Gass et al., 2013, p. 272-273). Rejects innate UG.
    • Mechanism: Learners learn language by "extracting regularities from the input." Grammar is "emergent" and based on frequency and exemplars.

3. Key Statistics

  • Over 50% of the world's population is bilingual or multilingual.
  • Monolingualism is the global minority condition.
  • Multilingualism provides economic advantages in globalized markets.

1. Key Concepts: Cognitive Architecture & Processing

  • Cognitive Architecture: How L1/L2 relate. Older views (Ervin & Osgood 1954): coordinate (separate), compound (fused), subordinate (L2 via L1). Modern view (Cook's multicompetence): an interconnected system, different from monolingualism. (See Languages & Brain modal for neuro details).
  • Executive Control: Higher-level processes (attention, inhibition, task switching). Bilinguals often show advantages ('bilingual advantage'), possibly due to managing two language systems. (See modal for details).
  • Working Memory (WM): System for temporarily holding and manipulating information. Crucial for language processing. Baddeley's model (Phonological Loop, Visuospatial Sketchpad, Central Executive) is influential. PSTM linked to L2 learning. (See modal for details).
  • Metalinguistic Awareness: Ability to reflect on language as an object. Bilinguals often show enhanced awareness (e.g., judging grammaticality) (Bialystok).
  • Processing Concepts (from IP):
    • Controlled vs. Automatic Processing: Learning moves from effortful attention to efficient skill use. (See IP modal).
    • Restructuring & U-Shaped Development: Learning involves reorganization, sometimes causing temporary errors (*foots*). (See IP modal).
    • Input, Intake, Output: Input needs **noticing** (intake). Output aids fluency, noticing gaps, etc. (See IP modal).

2. Brain Organization, Age Effects & Cognitive Benefits

  • Neural Plasticity & Brain Organization: Language primarily in **left hemisphere** (lateralization). Brain plasticity decreases with age. L1/L2 often overlap; organization varies (AoA, proficiency). (See modal for details).
  • Critical Period Hypothesis (CPH): (Lenneberg) Biological window for L1 due to plasticity. Speculated to affect L2 (esp. accent). Rate vs. Ultimate Attainment differences. (See modal for full details).
  • Cognitive Benefits of Bilingualism: Enhanced executive function; Increased creative/divergent thinking; Superior metalinguistic knowledge.
  • Cognitive Reserve: Bilingualism as mental exercise building brain resilience against age-related decline.

3. Individual Cognitive Differences

  • Language Aptitude: Specific talent (Carroll's components). Strong predictor of success (esp. rate). Related but distinct from general intelligence. (See modal for details).
  • Working memory capacity effects.
  • Learning Strategies: Conscious actions (Metacognitive, Cognitive, Social/Affective). Can guide attention to input (Schmidt 1990).
  • Learning Styles: Preferred ways of processing (e.g., Field Independence vs. Field Dependence). (See modal for details).
  • Intelligence: General cognitive ability (e.g., Gardner's Multiple Intelligences).

1. Key Concepts Covered

  • Sociocultural Theory (SCT) Concepts:
    • Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD): (Vygotsky) The distance between independent problem solving and potential development with guidance (Gass et al., 2013, p. 295).
    • Scaffolding: Support provided by a More Knowledgeable Other (MKO) within the ZPD.
    • Mediation: The core Vygotskyan concept that we do not interact with the world directly; our thought and action are "mediated" (shaped and organized) by cultural tools.
      • Tools: Can be physical (e.g., a pencil, a computer) or symbolic.
      • Language as the Key Tool: Language is the most powerful symbolic tool used to organize thought, plan actions, and share culture. Example: A teacher explaining a grammar rule uses language to *mediate* understanding.
    • Regulation: This refers to the process of gaining control over a complex task. Vygotsky saw it as moving through three stages:
      • Object-regulation: Using physical objects to manage a task (e.g., blocks to count).
      • Other-regulation: A person (MKO) guides the learner. This is **Scaffolding**.
      • Self-regulation: The learner takes control, often by using "private speech".
  • Communities of Practice (CoP): (Lave & Wenger) Groups defined by shared practices. Learning involves moving from Legitimate Peripheral Participation (LPP) to full participation.
  • Investment vs. Motivation: (Bonny Norton) Argues learners 'invest' in an L2 expecting a 'return,' influenced by Identity and Power Relations.
  • Power Relations: This explains how social hierarchies (e.g., teacher-student, boss-immigrant worker) can grant or deny a learner the **"right to speak"** (a key concept from Norton), which directly impacts their opportunity for practice and learning.

2. Classroom Social Dynamics

  • Teacher-student interaction: A primary source of comprehensible input and corrective feedback. The *quality* of this interaction (e.g., genuine questions vs. simple drills) is critical.
  • Peer interaction: Allows for co-construction of knowledge and **negotiation of meaning** (from Interactionist Theory) in a lower-anxiety setting.
  • Classroom culture: A positive, supportive culture lowers the **"Affective Filter"** (Krashen), encouraging learners to take risks without fear of negative evaluation.
  • Authentic vs. contrived communication: Authentic tasks (e.g., solving a real problem) are seen as more meaningful and motivating than 'drill and kill' exercises.

3. Social Factors in Natural Settings

  • Language contact situations: Provide massive amounts of naturalistic input, but the *type* of contact (e.g., friendly vs. hostile) determines its effectiveness.
  • Social network effects: A learner's social network (who they talk to) dictates the *type* and *amount* of L2 input and practice they get.
  • Community integration factors: Whether the learner *wants* to integrate (see Gardner's **"Integrative Motivation"**) and whether the target community *allows* them to integrate (see **"Power Relations"**).
  • Heritage language maintenance influences: Social and family pressure (or lack thereof) to maintain the L1 impacts identity and proficiency in both languages.

4. Social Identity Theory

  • Social identity construction: Identity isn't fixed; it's something we *perform*. The language we choose is a primary way we signal who we are.
  • Communities of practice: Membership in a CoP (like a 'sports team' or 'good students') is often tied to using the 'right' language, which links learning to belonging.
  • Identity negotiation: Learners negotiate their identity (e.g., "Am I still a [L1 identity] if I speak this L2? How do others see me when I speak?").
  • Power relations: As Norton argues, a learner may be "silenced" or their identity delegitimized by power structures, which causes them to resist learning.
  • Cultural-historical context: The *value* of a language (and the identity it represents) is shaped by its historical context (e.g., a 'colonial' language vs. an 'indigenous' one).

5. Key Theories

  • Sociocultural Theory (SCT): (Vygotsky) Higher mental functions originate in social interaction.
    • Key Concepts: ZPD, Scaffolding, Mediation
    • Internalization: Moving from the social (interpersonal) plane to the individual (intrapsychological) plane.
    • Private Speech: "Talking to oneself" as a tool for self-regulation.
  • Social Identity Theory: (Norton) Focuses on learners' identities, investment, and struggles over power.
  • Communities of Practice Theory: (Lave & Wenger) Learning is situated social participation.

1. Key Concepts: Ideologies & LPP

  • Language Ideologies: Beliefs about languages linked to power (Baker & Wright, 2017, p. 102).
    • Standard Language Ideology: Belief that one "standard" variety is superior, devaluing others.
    • Monolingual Ideology: Belief that monolingualism is the norm or ideal.
  • Language Policy (and Planning) (LPP): Explicit decisions about language use.
    • Status Planning: Changing the function of a language (e.g., official language).
    • Corpus Planning: Changing the structure of a language (e.g., standardizing spelling).

2. Educational Models

  • Transitional (TBE):
    • Goal: Assimilation into L2-only classrooms; *not* bilingualism.
    • Method: "Weak," subtractive model. L1 is used as a temporary bridge to learn the L2 (Baker & Wright, 2017).
    • Outcome: L1 support is removed once L2 proficiency is deemed sufficient. Often leads to L1 loss.
  • Maintenance (or Developmental):
    • Goal: Full bilingualism and biliteracy for language minority students.
    • Method: "Strong," additive model. Students receive instruction in *both* L1 and L2 for many years.
    • Outcome: Values and develops the L1 alongside the L2.
  • Two-Way Immersion (TWI) (or Dual Language):
    • Goal: Full bilingualism and biliteracy for *all* students.
    • Method: "Strong," additive model. Integrates language minority students (e.g., Spanish speakers) and language majority students (e.g., English speakers) in the same classroom.
    • Outcome: Both groups learn from each other, with all students becoming bilingual.
  • CLIL (Content and Language Integrated Learning):
    • Goal: To learn a subject *and* a language simultaneously.
    • Method: A form of immersion where subjects (e.g., biology, history) are taught *through* a non-native language.
    • Outcome: Content knowledge is gained while using the L2 in a meaningful, academic context.

3. Institutional Factors

  • Tracking and ability grouping:
    • Why it matters: This is the practice of separating students by perceived ability. Language minority students are at high risk of being **misdiagnosed**. A student still developing their L2 **CALP** (Cummins) may be wrongly placed in a "low-ability" track, denying them access to challenging content and creating a self-fulfilling prophecy.
  • Assessment practice appropriateness:
    • Why it matters: A standardized math test in a student's L2 is testing two things: **math knowledge AND language proficiency.** A student might fail because they don't understand the *language* of the test (the CALP), not because they don't understand the *math*. This is an invalid assessment.
  • Teacher preparation:
    • Why it matters: An unprepared teacher may operate from a "monolingual ideology," viewing the L1 as a "problem." A prepared teacher understands Cummins' **Interdependence Hypothesis**—that the L1 is a *resource* and a foundation for L2 learning.
  • Resource allocation and funding:
    • Why it matters: This connects LPP to reality. "Strong" additive models (like Maintenance) require bilingual teachers and materials. A lack of funding often forces schools into "weak" subtractive models, which **Skutnabb-Kangas** would call a form of systemic "linguistic genocide."

4. Sociopolitical Context

  • Language Rights: The idea that using/maintaining one's language is a fundamental human right.
  • Linguistic Genocide: (Skutnabb-Kangas) Policies (e.g., subtractive education) that aim for the "disappearance of a language" (Baker & Wright, 2017, p. 385).
  • Language Revitalization: Efforts to halt or reverse the decline of an endangered language (see Fishman's GIDS Scale).
  • Globalization and English dominance effects on local languages.

Languages and the Brain (Neurolinguistics)

Neurolinguistics explores how language is represented and processed in the brain. For most individuals, core language functions are lateralized to the left hemisphere, around the Sylvian fissure (including Broca's and Wernicke's areas), though many areas are involved. (Saville-Troike, pp. 72-73)

Multilingual Brain Organization

Research using brain damage studies, imaging (PET, fMRI), and other methods explores how multiple languages are organized. Key questions and findings (Saville-Troike, pp. 74-77):

  • How independent are the languages?

    Neither completely separate nor completely fused. May depend on context of learning (Ervin & Osgood's coordinate, compound, subordinate types) and proficiency level. Systems are interdependent but separable when needed (Obler & Gjerlow 1999). (p. 75)

  • Are languages stored in the same areas?

    Considerable individual variation exists. For some, L1 and L2 seem stored in slightly different, though overlapping, areas, primarily in the left hemisphere. The right hemisphere might be more involved in L2 than in L1. Some studies suggest L2 involves more diffuse activation, possibly indicating more reliance on memorized chunks initially (Fedio et al. 1992). (pp. 75-76)

  • Does organization differ with Age of Acquisition (AoA), learning method, or proficiency?

    Probably yes to all. Strongest evidence is for AoA: later learners show more right-hemisphere involvement (Vaid 1983; Wuillemin & Richards 1994). How L2 is learned might also affect brain organization (Cook 1992). (p. 76)

  • How are languages affected by brain damage?

    Mostly, languages are lost/recovered equally. When different, the language most used before the damage is often recovered first, not necessarily the L1. Different abilities (syntax vs. vocab, production vs. comprehension) can also be affected differently. (pp. 76-77)

Information Processing (IP) Framework

This psychological framework views L2 learning as the acquisition of a complex cognitive skill, similar to learning other skills (e.g., driving). It focuses on the mental processes involved. (Saville-Troike, p. 78, McLaughlin 1987)

Key Concepts & Assumptions (Saville-Troike, pp. 78-80)

  • Limited Capacity: Humans can only attend to a limited amount of new information at once.
  • Controlled vs. Automatic Processing:
    • Controlled: Initial learning requires conscious attention and effort. Slow, capacity-limited. Changeable.
    • Automatic: With practice, processes become faster, require less attention, and are harder to change. Frees up mental capacity for higher-level tasks.
  • Restructuring: Learning isn't just adding information; it involves reorganizing mental representations. This can lead to U-shaped development (e.g., *feet* -> *foots* -> *feet*) as learners apply new rules and then learn exceptions. (p. 80)
  • Stages of Processing (p. 79):
    • Input: Exposure to L2 samples.
    • Intake: Input must be noticed (attended to) to become available for processing. Factors influencing noticing include frequency, salience, instruction, ability, readiness, and task demands (Schmidt 1990).
    • Central Processing: Where learning occurs (automatization, restructuring). Can involve moving from declarative knowledge (facts, rules) to procedural knowledge (automated use) (Anderson 1976).
    • Output: Language produced by learners. Helps develop fluency (automatization), allows learners to notice gaps in knowledge, test hypotheses, and talk about language (Swain & Lapkin 1995). (pp. 79-80)
  • Automatization & Fossilization: If L2 forms become automatic before reaching target-like accuracy, they can become resistant to change (fossilization). (p. 79)

Related Models (Explaining Order of Acquisition)

  • Multidimensional Model / Processability Theory (Clahsen, Pienemann): Argue that developmental sequences reflect learners overcoming processing limitations. Simpler structures (e.g., canonical word order) are acquired before more complex ones requiring reordering or information exchange across phrases. Propose an implicational hierarchy of processing skills. (pp. 81-82)
  • Competition Model (MacWhinney, Bates): Views learning as mapping forms (sounds, words, morphemes, word order) to functions (meaning, grammatical roles). Learners detect cues in the input (e.g., word order, case marking, animacy) and learn their cue strength (reliability). SLA involves adjusting L1 cue strengths to L2. (pp. 82-84)

Critical Period Hypothesis (CPH)

Key Figure: Eric Lenneberg (1967)

Core Concept (Lenneberg, 1967)

Based on observations of L1 acquisition and brain development:

  • Children have a limited time (infancy to puberty) during which the brain retains plasticity, allowing them to acquire L1 flawlessly. Deprivation of input during this period (e.g., the case of Genie, ST p. 88) can lead to irreversible deficits.
  • Beyond this period, brain lateralization is complete, plasticity is reduced, and acquiring language becomes different.
  • Lenneberg speculated this applies to L2 as well: learning after puberty requires "a conscious and labored effort" rather than "automatic acquisition from mere exposure," explaining why later learners often retain a foreign accent. (ST, pp. 73, 89)

Primary Evidence (from PPTX)

  • Brain Plasticity & Lateralization: Language gradually specializes in the left hemisphere, solidifying at puberty.
  • Recovery from Aphasia: Children show much higher recovery rates than adults.

CPH Effects on L2 (from PPTX & ST)

  • Strongest Impact: Pronunciation (acquiring native-like accent).
  • Moderate Impact: Grammar (automatic acquisition becomes harder).
  • Least Impact: Vocabulary learning.

L2 Implications & Modifications (Saville-Troike, pp. 87-90)

  • Multiple Periods?: Sensitive periods might affect different aspects (phonology vs. syntax) differently (Seliger 1978; Long 1990).
  • Not Absolute Cut-off: Gradual decline; some older learners *can* achieve native-like proficiency.
  • Rate vs. Ultimate Attainment: Older learners often learn *faster* initially, but younger learners generally achieve *higher ultimate attainment*.
  • Context Matters: Younger advantage stronger in informal/naturalistic settings; older advantage in formal instruction.

Language Aptitude

A specific talent for language learning, composed of several cognitive abilities. Assumed to predict differential success in L2 learning. (Saville-Troike, p. 90)

It is the highest predictor among individual differences, predicting ~25% of variance in performance (PPTX, Dörnyei 2005).

Aptitude predicts the "rate of progress", not whether learning is ultimately possible (PPTX, Dörnyei 2005, p. 43).

Carroll's (1965) Four Components (Basis for MLAT) (Saville-Troike, pp. 90-91)

  • 1. Phonemic Coding Ability:

    Capacity to process auditory input into segments (phonemes) that can be stored and retrieved. Crucial for turning input into intake.

  • 2. Grammatical Sensitivity:

    Ability to recognize the grammatical function of elements in a sentence and understand patterns.

  • 3. Inductive Language Learning Ability:

    Ability to infer rules, identify patterns, and make generalizations from language samples.

  • 4. Associative Memory Capacity (Rote Learning):

    Ability to form and remember associations (e.g., L1-L2 vocab links); influences fluency.

Individual ability may vary by component. Aptitude is related to, but distinct from, general intelligence (ST p. 91).

Self-Determination Theory (SDT)

(Deci & Ryan, 1985; adapted for L2 by Noels et al., 2000, cited in PPTX). This theory proposes that motivation exists on a continuum of self-determination (autonomy). Higher self-determination leads to better quality motivation and better learning outcomes.

The Continuum of Self-Determination

(from least to most self-determined)

  • 1. Amotivation:

    No motivation. The learner sees no point in the activity. ("What's the point?")

  • 2. External Regulation (Extrinsic):

    Motivation comes purely from external rewards or punishments. ("Required for graduation.")

  • 3. Introjected Regulation (Extrinsic):

    Motivation comes from internal pressure, like guilt or shame. ("I'd feel guilty if I quit.")

  • 4. Identified Regulation (Extrinsic):

    The learner personally values the *outcome* of the activity, even if the activity itself isn't fun. ("Important for my career.")

  • 5. Intrinsic Motivation:

    Highest quality motivation; activity done for its own sake.

    • Knowledge: Joy of exploring new ideas.
    • Accomplishment: Satisfaction of mastering tasks.
    • Stimulation: Fun and excitement of the activity.

Personality Factors in SLA

Research on personality (using frameworks like the "Big Five": Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism; or MBTI) attempts to find traits predicting L2 success. However, results are weak and inconclusive (PPTX).

Why is the Research Inconclusive? (Dörnyei, via PPTX)

"Even carefully executed studies rarely manage to explain more than about 15% of the variance in academic success" (Dörnyei, 2005, p. 24)

“apart from a few exceptions, the SLA research community as a whole has given up on exploring how personality is implicated in the L2 learning process” (Dörnyei & Ryan, 2015, p. 28)

Dörnyei's "Cooking Recipe" Analogy (PPTX)

Why personality isn't a simple predictor:

"Personality traits can... be compared to the ingredients of a cooking recipe... a good cook can usually prepare a delicious meal of almost any ingredients" (Dörnyei, 2005, p. 28)

Meaning: A motivated learner with good strategies ("good cook") can succeed regardless of personality type ("ingredients").

Learning Styles (Field Independence / Dependence)

A "learning style" is a person's preferred or habitual way of processing information (Dörnyei 2005, cited in PPTX). Field Independence vs. Field Dependence is a key distinction:

1. Field Independence (FI)

  • Style: Analytic, detail-oriented.
  • Ability: Easily separates details from background ("sees the trees").
  • L2 Connection: May aid formal grammar learning.

2. Field Dependence (FD)

  • Style: Holistic, "big picture" oriented.
  • Ability: Perceives the whole field ("sees the forest"). More socially oriented.
  • L2 Connection: May aid communicative fluency.

Executive Control (Bilingual Advantage)

Executive Control (EC) refers to a set of high-level cognitive processes responsible for planning, problem-solving, and regulating thoughts and actions. Key components include:

  • Inhibition: Suppressing irrelevant information or automatic responses.
  • Working Memory Updating: Monitoring and updating information held in mind.
  • Shifting (Cognitive Flexibility): Switching between different tasks or mental sets.

The Bilingual Advantage Hypothesis (Primarily Bialystok)

This hypothesis proposes that the constant need for bilinguals to manage two languages provides intensive training for the brain's executive control system.

  • Mechanism: When speaking one language, the bilingual must constantly inhibit the non-target language. Switching between languages demands cognitive flexibility.
  • Evidence: Research often shows bilinguals outperform monolinguals on non-linguistic tasks measuring EC (e.g., Stroop task), suggesting transfer beyond language.
  • Caveats: The existence/size/universality of the advantage are still debated. Factors like age, proficiency, and frequency of switching may play a role.

Working Memory (WM) in Bilingualism

Working Memory (WM) is the cognitive system responsible for *temporarily storing* and *actively manipulating* information needed for complex tasks like reasoning, comprehension, and learning (Gass et al., 2013, p. 267).

Baddeley's Model (Commonly Used)

A widely accepted model conceptualizes WM with several components:

  • Phonological Loop: Briefly stores and rehearses verbal/auditory information. Crucial for language learning.
    • Phonological Short-Term Memory (PSTM): Part of the loop linked to success in learning L2 vocabulary and grammar (Gass et al., 2013, p. 269-270).
  • Visuospatial Sketchpad: Stores and manipulates visual/spatial information.
  • Central Executive: The attentional "supervisor"; coordinates components, allocates attention.
  • (Episodic Buffer: Integrates information into coherent episodes).

WM and Bilingualism

Managing two language systems demands WM resources. Research explores potential enhancements (like EC) or disadvantages (due to interference) depending on the task and bilingual experience.

Sociocultural Theory (SCT)

(Vygotsky, with key applications to SLA by Lantolf). This theory argues that learning is a social process first, and a cognitive one second. All higher mental functions, including language, originate in social interaction and are then "internalized" by the individual.

1. Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)

This is the 'sweet spot' for learning. It's the dynamic space between what a learner can do independently and what they can do with guidance. Learning doesn't happen when a task is too easy (boring) or too hard (frustrating); it happens when a task is in the ZPD and support is provided.

2. Scaffolding (or Other-regulation)

This is the action that happens in the ZPD. The 'More Knowledgeable Other' (MKO)—a teacher, parent, or more capable peer—provides temporary, adjustable support (e.g., prompting, questioning, modeling) to help the learner complete the task. The scaffolding is gradually removed as the learner becomes more competent.

3. Mediation

The central idea that humans don't just act on the world directly; our thinking is 'mediated' (shaped) by cultural tools (artifacts). Language is the single most important symbolic tool. We use it to organize our thoughts, plan our actions, and pass on culture. Learning is the process of mastering these tools.

4. Internalization & Private Speech

This is the final step: moving from the social plane (doing things with others, i.e., "other-regulation") to the individual plane (doing things on one's own, i.e., "self-regulation").

  • Private Speech: This is the key mechanism for internalization. It's "talking to oneself" (aloud or in one's head). An L2 learner might be observed muttering a grammar rule to themselves as they complete an exercise. This is Vygotsky's "thought-in-progress," where the learner is using language as a tool to mediate their own thinking and take control of the task.

Krashen's Monitor Model (The 5 Hypotheses)

This model (1980s) was highly influential, though not all parts are supported by modern research. (Gass et al., 2013, pp. 129-134; Ellis, 2015, p. 15, 26).

1. The Acquisition-Learning Hypothesis

This is the central hypothesis. It claims there are two independent systems:

  • Acquisition: The subconscious, "natural" process of "picking up" a language, just as children do with their L1. It's used for fluent communication.
  • Learning: The conscious, intentional process of "knowing about" a language (e.g., memorizing grammar rules).

2. The Monitor Hypothesis

This explains the relationship between the two systems. The "acquired" system is the source of all fluent output. The "learned" system acts only as an "inspector" or "Monitor" that checks and edits this output.

Three Conditions for Use: The Monitor can only be used when the learner:

  • Has sufficient Time.
  • Is Focused on Form (i.e., thinking about correctness).
  • Knows the Rule consciously.

3. The Natural Order Hypothesis

This claims that grammatical morphemes (e.g., plural *-s*, past tense *-ed*) are acquired in a predictable order, regardless of the learner's L1, age, or instruction.

4. The Input Hypothesis (Comprehensible Input)

This explains how acquisition happens. Acquisition occurs *only* when the learner receives comprehensible input (CI) that is slightly beyond their current level of competence (designated as $i+1$).

5. The Affective Filter Hypothesis

This explains why learners, even when given perfect "$i+1$" input, might not acquire it. Affective (emotional) factors like motivation, anxiety, and self-confidence create a "filter."

  • Low Filter: High motivation, low anxiety. Input passes through and becomes acquisition.
  • High Filter: Low motivation, high anxiety. Input is blocked, and acquisition does not occur.

Lave & Wenger's Communities of Practice (CoP)

(Sourced from Ellis, 2015, p. 232)

Core Idea

Learning is not just a cognitive process but a social one of "situated learning." It is the process of becoming a member of a community that shares common practices.

Key Concept: Legitimate Peripheral Participation (LPP)

Newcomers ("novices") learn by first observing and performing simple, low-risk tasks on the "periphery" of the community. As they gain expertise and become more integrated, they move toward "full participation" in the community's practices.

Norton's Investment Theory

(Sourced from Ellis, 2015, p. 230)

Core Idea

Replaces the static concept of "motivation" with the more dynamic, socially-aware concept of "investment." Learners "invest" in an L2 with the expectation of a "return."

Key Components

  • Identity: A learner's investment is tied to their multiple, complex, and often contradictory social identities (e.g., mother, worker, immigrant, student).
  • Power: The learner's "right to speak" is negotiated in interactions. Power relations (e.g., between an immigrant and an employer) can determine whether a learner is given opportunities to speak or is silenced, thus affecting their learning.
  • Agency: Learners are not just passive recipients; they are agents who can accept, resist, or reshape their social roles and identities in the process of learning.

Cummins' BICS/CALP & CUP Hypotheses

(Sourced from Baker & Wright, 2017, pp. 154-156)

BICS (Basic Interpersonal Communicative Skills)

  • The "surface" language of social conversation (the "tip of the iceberg").
  • It is context-embedded (supported by gestures, facial expressions).
  • Not cognitively demanding.
  • Acquired relatively quickly (approx. 2 years).

CALP (Cognitive Academic Language Proficiency)

  • The abstract, "school" language needed for academic success (the "submerged" part).
  • It is context-reduced (e.g., reading a textbook, writing an essay).
  • Cognitively demanding.
  • Takes much longer to acquire (5-7+ years).

CUP (Common Underlying Proficiency) / The Interdependence Hypothesis

This is the "Dual Iceberg Model." It argues that while the BICS (surface fluency) of L1 and L2 are separate, the CALP (academic skills, literacy, abstract concepts) is shared in one common "engine."

Key Implication: Strengthening the CALP in the L1 (e.g., teaching a child to read in Spanish) will directly transfer to and strengthen the CALP in the L2 (e.g., English). This is the central argument for maintenance bilingual education.

Bourdieu's Sociological Concepts

(Sourced from Baker & Wright, 2017, pp. 103, 307)

Cultural Capital

Non-financial assets (e.g., education, ways of speaking, style of dress, cultural knowledge) that are valued by a society's dominant institutions, especially schools.

Habitus

The set of deep-seated, socially learned dispositions, habits, and attitudes that shape how an individual perceives and acts in the world.

Legitimate Language / Symbolic Power

Dominant groups impose their language variety (e.g., "standard" English) as the "legitimate" or "correct" language. This grants it "symbolic power," making other varieties (e.g., dialects, heritage languages) seem "illegitimate" and thus turning linguistic differences into social inequality.

Gardner's Socio-Educational Model

(Sourced from Gardner, 1985; Gass et al., 2013, pp. 453-454; PPTX)

Core Principle (PPTX)

Language learning differs from other subjects because it requires incorporating elements of another culture. This makes social and cultural factors (like attitudes) central to language learning success.

The Integrative Motive (Key Components)

  • Integrativeness: The core concept. A positive disposition toward the L2 group and a genuine desire to interact with and even identify with them.
  • Attitudes toward the Learning Situation: The learner's feelings about the teacher, the course, and the learning environment.
  • Motivation (proper): The effort and desire to learn, combined with favorable attitudes.

Key Dichotomy

  • Integrative Motivation: The overall construct combining Integrativeness, Attitudes, and Motivation/Effort.
  • Instrumental Orientation: A practical, utilitarian reason for learning (e.g., to get a job, pass a test, fulfill a requirement).

Dörnyei's L2 Motivational Self System

(Sourced from Ellis, 2015, pp. 53-54)

Core Idea

A modern cognitive model of motivation based on future self-guides. Motivation is generated by the discrepancy between a learner's "actual self" and their "future selves."

Key Components

  • 1. Ideal L2 Self: The person the learner wants to become (e.g., a fluent, successful L2 speaker). This is a powerful "promotional" motive.
  • 2. Ought-to L2 Self: The attributes the learner believes they should possess, based on external expectations (e.g., from parents, teachers) to avoid negative outcomes. This is a "preventative" motive.
  • 3. L2 Learning Experience: The immediate, situational motivation related to the learning environment itself (the teacher, the materials, feelings of success).
