1. Key Concepts Covered

  • Second Language Acquisition (SLA):
    • Core Definition: The systematic process of learning languages beyond the mother tongue (L1).
    • Distinctions (from Gass et al., 2013, p. 4-5):
      • Second Language (L2): Learning a nonnative language in the environment where it is spoken (e.g., learning English in the US). Provides significant input.
      • Foreign Language (FL): Learning a nonnative language in the environment of one's native language (e.g., learning English in France). Input limited to classroom.
    • Acquisition vs. Learning: (See Krashen, below). 'Acquisition' = subconscious "picking up." 'Learning' = conscious "knowing about" rules. 'SLA' is often used as a general term for both.
  • Bilingualism: The use of two or more languages in daily life. Competence ranges from minimal to native-like.
    • Gass et al. (2013, p. 479) note this is a continuum, from minimal knowledge to the "mythical bilingual" with perfect native-like control.
  • Multilingualism: The global norm of speaking more than two languages.
  • Types of Bilingualism:
    • Simultaneous vs. Sequential (or Successive): Learning two languages from birth vs. learning an L2 after the L1 is established (Gass et al., 2013, p. 481).
    • Compound vs. Coordinate: Older distinction. Fused conceptual system (Compound) vs. separate systems (Coordinate). Current research (e.g., Cook's "multicompetence") suggests complex, interconnected systems in one mind.
    • Additive vs. Subtractive: Learning an L2 while maintaining L1 (Additive) vs. L2 replacing/weakening L1 due to social pressure (Subtractive) (Gass et al., 2013, p. 481, Table 15.1).

2. Historical Development & Key Theories

  • Behaviorist Theory (1950s-1960s):
    • Core Idea: Language learning is habit formation, shaped by imitation, practice, and reinforcement (Gass et al., 2013, p. 80-83).
    • Role of L1: Habits transfer. Positive transfer (facilitation) if same; negative transfer (interference) if different. Interference seen as source of all errors.
    • Associated Practice: Basis for Contrastive Analysis Hypothesis (CAH), which claimed L1/L2 comparison could predict all errors (Gass et al., 2013, p. 85).
    • Key Figure: B.F. Skinner (Verbal Behavior, 1957).
  • Mentalist Theory (1960s-1970s):
    • Core Idea: Rejection of Behaviorism. Learning is a creative, cognitive process. Humans have an innate Language Acquisition Device (LAD) and Universal Grammar (UG).
    • Evidence: L1 acquisition studies (e.g., Berko, 1958; Brown, 1973) showed children make systematic errors (e.g., *comed*) not in adult input, proving rule creation, not imitation (Gass et al., 2013, p. 109).
    • Key Figure: Noam Chomsky (1959 review of Skinner).
  • Interactionist Theory (1980s-present):
    • Core Idea: Middle-ground. Accepts internal mental processes (Mentalism) but argues they are activated by social interaction.
    • Key Concept: Negotiation of meaning (Gass et al., Ch. 12) during communication breakdowns is crucial for making input comprehensible and drawing attention to form.
    • Key Figure (related): Stephen Krashen (Monitor Model relies on comprehensible input).
  • Usage-based Theory (1990s-present):
    • Core Idea: Emergentist approach (Gass et al., 2013, p. 272-273). Rejects innate UG. Learners "extract regularities from the input."
    • Mechanism: Learning based on frequency. Grammar "emerges" from a complex network of connections built from experience.

3. Key Figures

  • B.F. Skinner: Leading proponent of Behaviorism (language as habit).
  • Noam Chomsky: Leading proponent of Mentalism (language as innate knowledge, UG).
  • François Grosjean: Key figure in bilingualism. Gass et al. (2013, p. 483) cite his work on "language mode," describing how bilinguals activate languages based on context (monolingual vs. bilingual mode), explaining code-switching.
  • Stephen Krashen: Proposed the influential . Gass et al. (2013, p. 129-134) detail its five hypotheses:
    • Acquisition-Learning Hypothesis: 'Acquisition' (subconscious) and 'learning' (conscious) are two independent systems.
    • Monitor Hypothesis: The 'learned' system acts as an "inspector" or "Monitor" to edit the 'acquired' system's output (requires time, focus on form, rule knowledge).
    • Natural Order Hypothesis: Grammatical morphemes are acquired in a predictable order, regardless of instruction.
    • Input Hypothesis (i+1): Acquisition occurs via comprehensible input slightly beyond the current level ($i+1$).
    • Affective Filter Hypothesis: Affective factors (anxiety, motivation) can "block" comprehensible input from reaching the LAD.

1. Key Concepts Covered

  • Cognitive Architecture: How L1 and L2 are represented (separate vs. shared).
    • Gass et al. (2013) note this is complex, often viewed as a continuum (p. 482). They cite Cook's (2003) concept of multicompetence: L1/L2 knowledge in one mind is a different state from monolingualism, forming an interconnected system.
  • Executive Control: Higher-level cognitive processes (attention, inhibition, switching). Bilinguals often show advantages ('bilingual advantage').
  • Working Memory (WM): The system for temporarily holding and manipulating information.
    • Gass et al. (2013, p. 267) define as "structures and processes that humans use to store and manipulate information."
    • Baddeley's Model: Includes phonological loop (verbal info), visuospatial sketch pad (visual info), and central executive (attentional "supervisor") (Gass et al., 2013, p. 267).
    • Phonological Short-Term Memory (PSTM): Linked to the phonological loop; research links it to successful L2 vocabulary and grammar learning (Gass et al., 2013, p. 269-270).
  • Metalinguistic Awareness: Ability to reflect on and manipulate language.
    • Gass et al. (2013, p. 482) note bilingual children often show advantages, citing Bialystok's work on "superior abilities in judging grammatical accuracy" and better "separating out individual words from meaningful sentences" compared to monolinguals.
  • Neural Plasticity & Brain Organization: Brain's ability to change in response to L2 learning.
    • Gass et al. (2013, p. 441) list "neurological changes" and "loss of plasticity" as a primary explanation for age-related differences.
  • Cognitive Reserve: Idea that bilingualism builds brain resilience, potentially delaying dementia.
  • Critical Period Hypothesis (CPH): (See Week 5) Theory that age affects language learning.
    • Gass et al. (2013, p. 434) define as a "limited developmental period during which it is possible to acquire a language... to normal, native-like levels," often linked to puberty.
    • Sensitive Period Hypothesis: A more gradual alternative; ability declines over time rather than stopping abruptly (p. 435).
    • Pronunciation is often affected first, then grammar.

2. Individual Cognitive Differences

  • (See Week 5) Specific talent for language learning. Measured by MLAT (John Carroll).
    • Gass et al. (2013, p. 445) detail its four components:
    • Phonemic coding ability: Discriminating and encoding foreign sounds.
    • Grammatical sensitivity: Recognizing word functions.
    • Inductive language-learning ability: Inferring rules from samples.
    • Rote-learning ability (Associative memory): Remembering L1-L2 vocabulary associations.
  • Learning Strategies: (See Week 5) Conscious actions to aid learning.
    • Gass et al. (2013, p. 468) cite O'Malley & Chamot's (1990) classification:
    • Metacognitive: Planning, monitoring, evaluating.
    • Cognitive: Repetition, note-taking, inferencing.
    • Social/Affective: Asking for clarification, managing anxiety.
  • Intelligence: General cognitive ability (distinct from aptitude).

3. Key Theories

  • Executive Control Theory: Bilingualism enhances executive functions (Ellen Bialystok).
  • Critical Period Hypothesis (CPH): Biological constraints limit ultimate attainment (Lenneberg).
  • Cognitive Reserve Theory: Bilingualism contributes to brain resilience.
  • Language Aptitude Theory: (Carroll) Specific cognitive abilities (measured by MLAT) predict success, particularly the rate of learning in formal settings.

1. Key Concepts Covered

  • Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD): (Vygotsky) Core SCT concept. Gass, Behney, & Plonsky (2013) cite Vygotsky's (1978) definition: "the distance between the actual developmental level... and the level of potential development as determined through problem solving under adult guidance or in collaboration with more capable peers" (p. 295). Learning occurs first on an interpersonal plane.
  • Scaffolding & Mediation:
    • Scaffolding: Verbal/non-verbal support from an MKO (More Knowledgeable Other) or peer to help a learner perform a task in their ZPD.
    • Mediation: Broader SCT concept. Gass et al. (2013) explain all human activity is "mediated" by tools (material artifacts or symbolic artifacts like language). Language is the primary symbolic tool shaping thought.
    • Regulation: A form of mediation moving through 3 stages (Gass et al., 2013):
      • Object-regulation: Using objects to manage a task.
      • Other-regulation: A person guides the learner (scaffolding).
      • Self-regulation: Learner takes control, often via Private Speech.
  • (Lave & Wenger) Groups defined by shared practices. Learning involves moving from legitimate peripheral participation (newcomer) towards full participation.
  • (Bonny Norton) Argues 'motivation' is insufficient. Learners 'invest' in an L2 expecting a 'return' (social, economic). Linked to identity and power relations.
  • Social Identity Construction: Identity is dynamic, multiple, and socially constructed through language use.
  • Power Relations: Social hierarchies affecting learners' access, opportunities, and investment.

2. Social Dynamics and Factors

  • Classroom Dynamics: Teacher-student and peer interactions, classroom culture.
  • Natural Settings: Language contact, social networks, community integration.

3. Key Theories

  • Sociocultural Theory (SCT): (Vygotsky) Higher mental functions originate in social interaction and are mediated by cultural tools. Key concepts:
    • ZPD, Scaffolding, Mediation (see above).
    • Internalization: Moving from the social (interpersonal) plane to the individual (intrapsychological) plane (Gass et al., 2013, p. 294).
    • Private Speech: "Talking to oneself" (Vygotsky, 1987) as a tool for self-regulation. Used by L2 learners (Gass et al., 2013, p. 295). A key mechanism of internalization.
  • Social Identity Theory: (Norton) L2 learning relates to learners' identities, investment, and power struggles.
  • Communities of Practice Theory: (Lave & Wenger) Learning is situated social participation.
  • Investment Theory: (Norton) Explains engagement via identity, investment, and power.

4. Key Figures

  • Lev Vygotsky: Foundational figure for Sociocultural Theory (SCT).
  • Bonny Norton: Investment and Social Identity theories.
  • Jean Lave & Etienne Wenger: Situated Learning and Communities of Practice.
  • James Lantolf: Applied Vygotsky's SCT to SLA research.

1. Key Concepts Covered

  • Language Ideologies:
    • Definition: Baker & Wright (2017) describe as "a set of beliefs and ideas... linked to the distribution of power and resources" (p. 102) that "justify and rationalize existing social and power structures."
  • Standard Language Ideology:
    • Definition: Belief that a single, standardized form ("school" language) is inherently superior and more "correct" than other varieties (dialects).
    • Impact: "Privileges the language of dominant, powerful groups" and devalues varieties of subordinate groups in schools (Baker & Wright, 2017, p. 102-103).
  • Monolingual Ideology (or "Monolingual Mindset"):
    • Definition: Belief that monolingualism is the norm or ideal. Treats bilingualism as a "problem to be remedied" not a resource (Baker & Wright, 2017, p. 306).
  • Language Policy (and Planning) (LPP):
    • Definition: Explicit decisions to influence language use, status, and acquisition.
    • Types (Baker & Wright, 2017, p. 110):
      • Status Planning: Changing the use/function of a language (e.g., making it official).
      • Corpus Planning: Changing the structure of a language (e.g., standardizing spelling).
  • Educational Models for Bilingual Learners:
    • Baker & Wright (2017, Ch. 11) categorize into "Weak" (Subtractive) and "Strong" (Additive) forms.
    • Transitional (TBE): Weak/subtractive. L1 used as temporary bridge to L2-only instruction. Goal is assimilation, not bilingualism (p. 211).
    • Maintenance (Developmental): Strong/additive. Aims for full bilingualism/biliteracy for language minority students via L1+L2 instruction (p. 216).
    • Two-Way Immersion (TWI) / Dual Language: Strong/additive. Integrates language minority and majority students; instruction in both languages for all (p. 222).
    • CLIL: Content and Language Integrated Learning. Teaching subject matter (e.g., science) through a non-native language (p. 219).
  • Language Rights:
    • Definition: The idea that using/maintaining one's language is a fundamental human right, essential for cultural identity (Baker & Wright, 2017, Ch. 18).
  • Linguistic Genocide:
    • Definition: (Tove Skutnabb-Kangas) "any action that has as its aim or outcome the disappearance of a language" (Baker & Wright, 2017, p. 385). Often accomplished via subtractive education policies.
  • Language Revitalization:
    • Definition: Efforts to reverse the decline of an endangered language (Baker & Wright, 2017, p. 57), linked to Fishman's "Reversing Language Shift" (RLS).
    • Fishman's GIDS Scale: Tool to classify language health based on intergenerational transmission.

2. Key Theories

  • Language Ideology Theory: Analyzes how beliefs about language legitimize social and linguistic hierarchies (see above).
  • Language Policy and Planning Theory: Studies the processes and outcomes of language-related decision-making.
  • Language Rights Theory: Advocates for linguistic rights based on human rights principles (see Skutnabb-Kangas).
  • Critical Theory (): Baker & Wright (2017, p. 103, 307) apply this: schools value the "cultural capital" and "habitus" (dispositions, language use) of the dominant class. "Standard" language = "legitimate language" with "symbolic power," devaluing minority students' language capital.

1. Key Concepts Covered

  • Age Effects (CPH): (See Week 2).
    • Gass et al. (2013, p. 435-436) note younger learners (children) generally achieve higher *ultimate attainment* (esp. phonology), but older learners (adults) are often faster in *initial stages* (rate).
    • Ellis (2015, Ch. 2) confirms this: older learners have cognitive maturity (rate advantage), but long-term disadvantage in ultimate attainment (esp. in naturalistic settings).
  • Personality Factors: (Mixed/weak findings)
    • Extroversion vs. Introversion: Gass et al. (2013, p. 465) and Ellis (2015, p. 49-50) agree results are "mixed and inconclusive."
    • Risk-taking: Potentially beneficial.
    • Anxiety: Specifically Language Anxiety.
      • Gass et al. (2013, p. 462) note a possible "curvilinear effect: low levels help, whereas high levels hurt."
      • Ellis (2015, p. 50, citing Horwitz) identifies sources: communication apprehension, fear of negative evaluation, test anxiety. Can be debilitative (harmful) or facilitative (motivating).
    • Self-esteem & Confidence: Often correlates positively.
  • Motivation Types:
    • Integrative: (Gardner) "disposition toward the L2 group and the extent to which he or she desires to interact with... that group" (Gass et al., 2013, p. 453).
    • Instrumental: (Gardner) Learning for practical goals (job, grades) (Gass et al., 2013, p. 453).
    • Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic: (Deci & Ryan - Self-Determination Theory)
      • Intrinsic: "motivation to engage in an activity because it is enjoyable and satisfying" (Gass et al., 2013, p. 450).
      • Extrinsic: "actions carried out to achieve some instrumental end" (p. 450).
  • Cognitive Differences:
    • Aptitude: (Carroll) Specific cognitive abilities (phonemic coding, etc.). Ellis (2015, p. 47) notes it's a "strong predictor of success... especially when learning takes place in a formal classroom setting."
    • Learning Strategies & Styles: Strategies = specific actions; Styles = broad preferences. Strategies are "consciously selected by learners" to "enhance the learning" (Gass et al., 2013, p. 467, citing Cohen).
    • Multiple Intelligences: (H. Gardner) e.g., linguistic, logical, spatial, etc.

Krashen's Monitor Model (The 5 Hypotheses)

This model (from the 1980s) was one of the first major theories of SLA. It is highly influential, though not all parts are supported by modern research. (Sourced from Gass, Behney, & Plonsky, 2013, pp. 129-134; Ellis, 2015, p. 15, 26).

  • The Acquisition-Learning Hypothesis: This is the central hypothesis. It claims there are two independent systems:
    • Acquisition: The subconscious, "natural" process of "picking up" a language, just as children do with their L1. It's used for fluent communication.
    • Learning: The conscious, intentional process of "knowing about" a language (e.g., memorizing grammar rules).
  • The Monitor Hypothesis: The "acquired" system is the source of all fluent output. The "learned" system acts only as an "inspector" or "Monitor" that checks and edits this output.
    • Three Conditions for Use: The Monitor can only be used when the learner: (1) Has sufficient Time, (2) Is Focused on Form, and (3) Knows the Rule consciously.
  • The Natural Order Hypothesis: Claims that grammatical morphemes (like plural -s, past tense -ed) are acquired in a predictable order, regardless of L1, age, or instruction.
  • The Input Hypothesis (i+1): Acquisition happens only when the learner receives comprehensible input (CI) that is slightly beyond their current level (i + 1).
  • The Affective Filter Hypothesis: Affective (emotional) factors like motivation and anxiety create a "filter." If the filter is high (high anxiety), it blocks input, and acquisition does not occur.

Carroll's Model of Language Aptitude

This model defines L2 aptitude (natural talent for language learning) as a stable, cognitive trait composed of four specific, independent abilities. It is measured by the Modern Language Aptitude Test (MLAT). (Sourced from Gass et al., 2013, p. 445; Ellis, 2015, p. 53).

  • Phonemic Coding Ability: The ability to identify distinct sounds, form associations between sounds and written symbols, and retain these associations.
  • Grammatical Sensitivity: The ability to recognize the grammatical function of words in a sentence (e.g., identifying the subject, verb) without necessarily knowing the explicit rule.
  • Inductive Language-Learning Ability: The ability to identify patterns and infer rules from a given language sample.
  • Rote-Learning Ability (or Associative Memory): The ability to form and remember associations between new words (or sounds) and their meanings (e.g., L1-L2 vocabulary links).

Lave & Wenger's Communities of Practice (CoP)

(Sourced from Ellis, 2015, p. 232)

  • Core Idea: Learning is not just a cognitive process but a social one of "situated learning." It is the process of becoming a member of a community that shares common practices.
  • Key Concept: Legitimate Peripheral Participation (LPP): Newcomers ("novices") learn by first observing and performing simple, low-risk tasks on the "periphery" of the community. As they gain expertise, they move toward "full participation."

Norton's Investment Theory

(Sourced from Ellis, 2015, p. 230)

  • Core Idea: Replaces "motivation" with "investment." Learners "invest" in an L2 with the expectation of a "return."
  • Identity: A learner's investment is tied to their multiple, complex social identities (e.g., mother, worker, immigrant).
  • Power: The learner's "right to speak" is negotiated. Power relations (e.g., immigrant and employer) can determine if a learner is given opportunities to speak or is silenced.
  • Agency: Learners are not passive; they are agents who can accept, resist, or reshape their social roles and identities.

Cummins' BICS/CALP & CUP Hypotheses

(Sourced from Baker & Wright, 2017, pp. 154-156)

  • BICS (Basic Interpersonal Communicative Skills):
    • The "surface" language of social conversation.
    • Context-embedded (supported by gestures, expressions). Not cognitively demanding.
    • Acquired quickly by immigrant children (approx. 2 years). This is the "tip of the iceberg."
  • CALP (Cognitive Academic Language Proficiency):
    • The abstract, "school" language needed for academic success.
    • Context-reduced (e.g., reading a textbook). Cognitively demanding.
    • Takes much longer to acquire (5-7+ years). This is the "submerged" part of the iceberg.
  • CUP (Common Underlying Proficiency) / The Interdependence Hypothesis:
    • The "Dual Iceberg Model." While BICS (surface fluency) of L1 and L2 are separate, the CALP (academic skills, concepts) is shared in one common "engine."
    • Key Implication: Strengthening CALP in L1 (e.g., reading in Spanish) directly transfers to and strengthens CALP in L2 (e.g., English). This is the central argument for maintenance bilingual education.

Bourdieu's Sociological Concepts

(Sourced from Baker & Wright, 2017, pp. 103, 307)

  • Cultural Capital: Non-financial assets (e.g., education, ways of speaking, cultural knowledge) valued by a society's dominant institutions, especially schools.
  • Habitus: The deep-seated, socially learned dispositions, habits, and attitudes that shape how an individual perceives and acts in the world.
  • Legitimate Language / Symbolic Power: Dominant groups impose their language variety (e.g., "standard" English) as the "legitimate" language. This grants it "symbolic power," making other varieties (e.g., heritage languages) seem "illegitimate" and turning linguistic differences into social inequality.

Gardner's Socio-Educational Model

(Sourced from Gass et al., 2013, pp. 453-454; Ellis, 2015, pp. 59-60)

  • Core Idea: A foundational social-psychological model explaining L2 learning success.
  • Integrativeness: The core concept. A positive disposition toward the L2 group and a genuine desire to interact with and even identify with them.
  • Attitudes toward the Learning Situation: The learner's feelings about the teacher, the course, and the learning environment.
  • Motivation (proper): The effort and desire to learn, combined with favorable attitudes.
  • Integrative Motivation: The overall construct combining Integrativeness, Attitudes, and Motivation/Effort.
  • Instrumental Orientation: A practical, utilitarian reason for learning (e.g., to get a job, pass a test). Gardner found this was also a component, but often less stable than integrativeness.

Dörnyei's L2 Motivational Self System

(Sourced from Ellis, 2015, pp. 53-54)

  • Core Idea: A modern cognitive model of motivation based on future self-guides. Motivation is generated by the discrepancy between a learner's "actual self" and their "future selves."
  • 1. Ideal L2 Self: The person the learner *wants* to become (e.g., a fluent, successful L2 speaker). A powerful "promotional" motive (what you want to achieve).
  • 2. Ought-to L2 Self: The attributes the learner believes they *should* possess, based on external expectations (e.g., from parents, teachers) to avoid negative outcomes. A "preventative" motive (what you need to do to avoid failure).
  • 3. L2 Learning Experience: The immediate, situational motivation related to the learning environment itself (the teacher, the materials, feelings of success).
